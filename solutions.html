Solutions and solubilities

Solubility

The solubility of a solid in a liquid is the number of moles of it that would dissolve in 1 dm3 of the liquid, in the presence of its excess solute, at a given temperature.

Solution of gases in liquids

Solutions of gases in liquids and their respective solubilities also depend on factors like temperature and pressure. Many natural occurences in life illustrate this phenomenon. It is known, for example that there is a remarkable increase in solubility of nitrogen in the blood of deep sea divers while they are being subjected to very high pressures under water. Any sudden return to atmospheric pressure on the part of divers results into pain in their bodies as the excess air is forcibly released. This condition is known as the "bends". A gradual return to atmospheric pressure releases air gently, which is not painful.Soda water is a result of caerbon dioxide dissolving in water at a pressure higher than atmosphere and the gradual release of gases as soda water is being drunk gives it characteristic taste.

True solutions and suspensions

We have seen a salt like potassium nitrate, or a liquid like ethanol, dissolves in water, a homogeneous solution is made.The particles of the solute disappear and obviously cannot be recovered by ordinary filteration. Such a solution called a true solution
All solutions are however not true solutions.
A suspension is a mixture of a finely divided solid and a liquid in which it will not dissolve. A difference between solutions and suspensions is due to the relative sizes of the particles.The particles in a solution are individual ions or molecules which will not settle out and cannot be filtered off, whereas those of a suspension can be retained by a filter paper. It is also possible to have have suspensions of solids, gases and liquids in either liquids or gases.

Some common examples of suspensions

  1. Solid aerosol is a suspension of solid particles in a gas e.g smoke
  2. Liquid aerosol is a suspension of liquid particles in a gas e.g fog
  3. The Harmattan is caused by a suspension of solid particles, mainly small sand particles, in air
  4. An emulsion is a suspension of liquid particles in another liquid with which it is immiscible, e.g oil and water as in cold-liver oil and stiff grease.
  5. Foams are suspensions of gases in liquids. Here liquid films encircle globules of gas as in a soap lather or in Some fire extinguishers

    6. Between a true solution and suspension lies another kind of solution called a sol.
